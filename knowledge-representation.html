Knowledge Representation

through the description of data and RDF

Data description

In the description of data, we have made a selection of the entities, objects and their relations to be represented according to the conceptual model: we have chosen the most representative of our idea and our project. It is the event, its four entities (place, time, concept and person) and one significant item for each entity, keeping separate the descriptions of the entities from those of the objects we have considered.

As a type of representation, we chose the tables and the natural language. The first because they are an intermediate data structure between a visual representation such as the graph of the conceptual model and the organization in triples of the RDF (the three columns correspond to the subject, the predicate and the object). And the natural language to describe subjects and objects of the triples seemed to us the best choice for a double reason: first, as an intermediate passage between the formal language of ontologies and the specific lexicon of the RDF; secondly, to move from the generality of classes and properties of ontologies to the specificity of the URIs and literals of the RDF.

RDF

For the rdf description representation of data, we have chosen those entities and items that were suitable for creating relevant interconnections, representative of the event itself: the photograph, the event, the concept, the movie, a person, the judgment and the association.

This choice has allowed us to build an RDF in which the objects of the triples become in turn subjects, enhancing the links within our knowledge model, then recovering the relationships between items and entities already prefigured in the conceptual model and in the data description.

As serialization of RDF we chose Turtle for its clarity, thus for its support for namespace prefixes and its way to abbreviate information related to triples, for example by factoring out common portions of the URIs.

All the subjects of the triples have been connected to objects represented both with external and internal resources. When it was possible, the external resources have been identified through already existent URIs. Instead, we mint our URIs for those objects and entities belonging to our domain, when they were also subjects of triples.

We also provided a visual representation of the RDF graph in directed-arc diagrams (in which each triple is represented as a node-arc-node link). This passage is meant to conclude the RDF and look at it from its graphical aspect, in order to enhance, on a visual level, its internal and external connection.
